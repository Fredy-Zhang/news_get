class="entry-content"><p>This weekend the World Health Organization (WHO) had to delete a misleading tweet about the coronavirus. Unfortunately, several media outlets had already cited it, spreading unwarranted fear about the likelihood of secondary COVID-19 infections.</p><p>On Friday, the WHO published a scientific brief on "<a href="https://www.who.int/news-room/commentaries/detail/immunity-passports-in-the-context-of-covid-19">immunity passports</a>"—the idea that governments should grant special documents to citizens who test positive for COVID-19 antibodies, allowing them to move about freely. The WHO warned that this is premature, since "no study has evaluated whether the presence of antibodies to SARS-CoV-2 confers immunity to subsequent infection by this virus in humans."</p><p>The WHO is correct that scientists have not determined the degree of immunity enjoyed by COVID-19 survivors. But <a href="https://static.abplive.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/26153747/WHO-d.jpg?impolicy=abp_cdn&amp;imwidth=640">the tweet version</a> of the brief was missing important context, and it said only this: "There is currently no evidence that people who have recovered from #COVID19 and have antibodies are protected from a second infection."</p><p>That's technically true: There's no evidence of immunity. But that's because COVID-19 is new and the matter hasn't been conclusively studied yet. Scientists have good reason to expect COVID-19 survivors to have <em>some </em>immunity to the virus, though they're unsure how strong it will be or how long it will last.</p><p>"When they say 'no evidence' they mean something like 'no definitive proof, yet,'" <a href="https://twitter.com/NateSilver538/status/1254130268732100609">wrote</a> statistician Nate Silver in response to the WHO tweet. "But the average person is going to read it as 'there's no immunity to coronavirus,' which is likely false and not a good summation of the evidence."</p><p>Indeed, <em>Bloomberg News </em><a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-04-25/catching-covid-19-may-not-shield-against-new-infection-who-says?utm_campaign=socialflow-organic&amp;cmpid=socialflow-twitter-business&amp;utm_source=twitter&amp;utm_medium=social&amp;utm_content=business">reported</a> this story with the headline "WHO Warns You May Catch Coronavirus More Than Once." Here's how the article started:</p><blockquote><p>Catching Covid-19 once may not protect you from getting it again, according to the World Health Organization, a finding that could jeopardize efforts to allow people to return to work after recovering from the virus.</p></blockquote><p>That's just wrong. There is no "finding" to speak of here, just an absence of definitive proof that antibodies confer a degree of immunity. Many readers undoubtedly would come away from these statements with a level of anxiety—<em>You can </em><em>get it again!
