					    <img src="/sites/default/files/nfs/diff-intra/english/shared/top/18859c67cf283399c568e9ad780ff60d47e81f6e.jpg" class="w100" />
					    <span>AFP / Miguel MEDINA</span>
					    <span class="copyright_under"><strong>The use of public transport will be discouraged and everyone will have to wear masks in indoor public spaces</strong></span>
				    </div><p>Stir-crazy Italians were free to stroll and visit relatives for the first time in nine weeks on Monday as Europe's hardest-hit country eased back the world's longest remaining coronavirus lockdown.</p><p>More than four million people -- an estimated 72 percent of them men -- returned to their construction sites and factories as the economically and emotionally shattered country tried to get back to work.</p>
			  </div><p>The sounds of banging and drilling echoed across Rome and a group of men drank espresso out of plastic cups in front of the Pantheon, the former Roman temple, as cafes reopened for takeout service.</p><p>"We can hear more noise now," Rome grocery story owner Daniela observed. "It's better than this frightening silence."</p><p>But bars and even ice cream parlours will remain shut. The use of public transport will be discouraged and everyone will have to wear masks in indoor public spaces.</p><p>- 'Moment of responsibility' -</p><p>Italy became the first Western democracy to shut down virtually everything in the face of an illness that has now officially killed 28,884 -- the most in Europe -- and some fear thousands more.</p><p>The lives of Italians began closing in around them as it became increasingly apparent that the first batch of infections in provinces around Milan were spiralling out of control.</p><p>Prime Minister Giuseppe Conte began by putting a quarter of the population in the northern industrial heartland on lockdown on March 8.</p>
        <div class="line textcontent_img watermark">
				    <div class="w50 right ml1" >
					   <img src="/sites/default/files/nfs/diff-intra/english/shared/top/728a986b419752451c6506f46fb8ec3f558d3c66.jpg" class="w100" />
					   <span>AFP / Andreas SOLARO</span>
					    <span class="copyright_under"><strong>Italy became the first Western democracy to shut down virtually everything in the face of the illness</strong><br/></span>
				    </div><p>The sudden measure frightened many -- fearful of being locked in together with the gathering threat -- into fleeing to less affected regions further south.</p><p>The danger of the virus spreading with them and incapacitating the south's less developed health care system forced Conte to announce a nationwide lockdown on March 9.</p>
			  </div><p>"Today is our moment of responsibility," Conte told the nation.</p><p>The official death toll was then 724.</p><p>More waves of restrictions followed as hundreds began dying each day.</p><p>Almost everything except for pharmacies and grocery stores was shuttered across the Mediterranean country of 60 million on March 12.</p><p>Conte's final roll of the dice involved closing all non-essential factories on March 22.</p><p>Italy's highest single toll -- 969 -- was reported five days later.</p><p>- 'Worried about reopening' -</p><p>The economic toll of all those shutdowns has been historic.</p><p>Italy's economy -- the eurozone's third-largest last year -- is expected to shrink more than in any year since the global depression of the 1930s.</p><p>Half of the workforce is receiving state support and the same number told a top pollster that they were afraid of becoming unemployed.</p><p>And some of those who are out of a job already say they do not entirely trust in Conte's ability to safely navigate the nation out of peril.</p>
        <div class="line textcontent_img watermark">
				    <div class="w50 left mr1" >
					    <img src="/sites/default/files/nfs/diff-intra/english/shared/top/794b7d1c6e9aecbf9cda395fed7024999b997e27.jpg" class="w100" />
					    <span>AFP / Miguel MEDINA</span>
					    <span class="copyright_under"><strong>More than four million people returned to their construction sites and factories as the economically and emotionally shattered country tried to get back to work</strong></span>
				    </div><p>Conte's popularity has jumped along with that of most other world leaders grappling with the pandemic thanks to a 'rally around the flag' effect.</p><p>But a Demos poll conducted at the end of April found some of Conte's lustre fading.</p>
			  </div><p>"I am worried about the reopening. The authorities seem very undecided about how to proceed<em>,</em>" 37-year-old Davide Napoleoni told AFP.</p><p>Confidence in his government has slipped by eight percentage points to a still-strong 63 percent since March.</p><p>- Psychological toll -</p><p>Italy's staggered reopening is complicated by a highly decentralised system that allows the country's 20 regions to layer on their own rules.</p><p>Venice's Veneto and the southern Calabria regions have thus been serving food and drink at bars and restaurants with outdoor seating since last week.</p><p>The area around Genoa is thinking of allowing small groups of people to go sailing and reopening its beaches.</p><p>Neighbouring Emilia-Romagna is keeping them closed -- even to those who live by the sea.</p><p><a class="videoSet"><a class="photo" href="#photo3" style="rightSide"/><a class="video" href="../../video/jpg/963cd89c-08e8-45b1-be99-46aa1ac18849.jpg" title="jpeg"/><a class="video" href="../../video/mpg/963cd89c-08e8-45b1-be99-46aa1ac18849.1920x1080.mp4" title="Mpeg4-1920x1080"/><a class="video" href="../../video/mpg/963cd89c-08e8-45b1-be99-46aa1ac18849.1280x720.mp4" title="Mpeg4-1280x720"/><a class="video" href="../../video/qt-hi/963cd89c-08e8-45b1-be99-46aa1ac18849.640x360.mp4" title="Mpeg4-640x360"/></a>All this uncertainty appears to be weighing on the nation's psyche.</p><p>A poll by the Piepoli Institute showed 62 percent of Italians think they will need psychological support with coming to grips with the post-lockdown world.</p><p>"The night of the virus continues," sociologist Ilvo Diamanti wrote in La Repubblica daily.</p><p>"And you can hardly see the light on the horizon. If anything, we're getting used to moving in the dark."</p>    </div>
