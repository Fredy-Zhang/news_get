<div class="article-title"><h1>
Scientists Capture Incredibly Rare Video of Humpback Whales Nursing Their Calves </h1></div>
<div class="author-name">
<div class="author-name-image"></div>

<div id="item_43964" class="article-mainbody-container">
<div class="article-mainbody-centre">
<div class="article-fulltext">
<p>Whales, as we all know, are different from most other sea creatures. They are mammals, and give birth to live babies… and then they nurture and raise those babies, nursing them on rich, fatty milk so they can grow big and strong.</p><div class="priad" id='Purch_Y_C_0_1'><script>tmntag.cmd.push(function(){tmntag.adTag('Purch_Y_C_0_1', false);});</script></div>
<div class=priad-mobile-spacer>&nbsp;</div><p>But we can't just put whales in an aquarium and watch them raise their families. They are large beasts with complex societies, and their migration can take them around the world.</p><p>While the ocean isn't exactly hospitable to humans, though, we can send our technology. Through the use of whale-mounted cameras, scientists from the University of Hawaiʻi, Stanford University, and UC Santa Cruz were able to capture breathtaking video of humpback whales nursing their calves as they wintered in their breeding grounds in Maui.</p><p>Every year, around 10,000 humpback whales return to these warm, shallow waters to birth their young. The mothers will then spend a few months - from January to March - raising their calves, strengthening them for the migration to their krill-rich summer feeding grounds in Alaska.</p><p>During these winter months, the mothers don't eat, living - and feeding their calves - off their blubber stores. What the research team wanted to know is how often and for how long the calves feed, in order to be strong enough for the spring migration.</p><p><iframe src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/EUcMuUBMYJc" width="700" height="414" allowfullscreen="allowfullscreen"></iframe></p><p>They put a number of technologies to work. Non-invasive sensor arrays - including acoustic recorders, accelerometers, pressure sensors and, of course, cameras - were attached to seven humpback whale calves via suction cups.</p><p>The team also used drones to fly over and observe the whales from above.</p><div class="priad" id='Purch_Y_C_0_1_clone_1'>
