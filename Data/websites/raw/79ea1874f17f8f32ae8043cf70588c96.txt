<div class="text-body"><p>Right-wing voices around the world have been playing a disproportionate role in spreading coronavirus misinformation and conspiracy theories, a new report has found.&nbsp;</p><p><a href="https://public-assets.graphika.com/reports/Graphika_Report_Covid19_Infodemic.pdf" target="_blank">The report by Graphika</a>, a US-based company specialising in online marketing and social media analysis, is based on a project mapping online COVID-19 misinformation.&nbsp;</p><p>The company’s work - based on data collected primarily from Twitter in January, February and March - found right-wing voices, partly fuelled by a “mega cluster” in the United States, spread nearly four times as much disinformation as left-leaning voices.</p><p>“Graphika’s dedicated coronavirus disinformation map, seeded on disinformation-specific hashtags, reveals that while conservative groups have a larger total presence than liberal groups, the combined volume of activity from conservative groups is 27 per cent, compared with eight per cent from left-leaning groups,” the report said.</p><p><div class="block__container"><div class="label">READ MORE</div><div class="preview__list-wrapper"><div class="preview preview--linear"><div class="preview__wrap"><div class="media-image media-image--1-1 preview__image"><img src="https://sl.sbs.com.au/public/image/file/a5fa3a3a-c0a6-4955-9fb3-f74ee922dc6c/crop/1x1" alt="People wearing masks in Sydney's CBD."/></div><div class="preview__content"><h3 class="headline preview__headline"><a href="/news/warnings-australia-s-far-right-could-exploit-the-coronavirus-pandemic" title="Warnings Australia's far-right could exploit the coronavirus pandemic">Warnings Australia's far-right could exploit the coronavirus pandemic</a></h3></div></div></div></div></div></p><p>“This indicates not only that there are more right-wing accounts, but these accounts are also more prolifically producing content than their left-wing counterparts.”</p><p>Right-wing groups in the US, Italy, and France were more actively spreading misinformation, with left-wing groups more active in other EU countries and Latin American countries, the report said.&nbsp;</p><p>Graphika's analysis found “a high proportion of conspiracy-focused content” online, which at first appeared to revolve around the causes of the coronavirus outbreak, but later shifted towards government responses.</p><p>“As the pandemic continued to spread to geographies in which communities that support these theories are particularly strong, conspiratorial content has become more closely focused on governmental responses to the outbreak,” the report said.&nbsp;</p><h3><strong>Racism and anti-immigration sentiment</strong></h3><p>The report also found a number of groups have been attempting to leverage the online conversation around the coronavirus pandemic to cultivate racism and anti-immigration sentiment.</p><p>“Some of these communities focus upon the origins of the outbreak to spread anti-Chinese content, but the French and Italian right-wing are determined to draw attention to immigration policy in their respective countries,” the report said.</p><p>“This content is most often shared via articles on clickbait ‘news’ sites and expertly blends conspiratorial narratives with anti-establishment messaging.”</p><p><div class="block__container"><div class="label">READ MORE</div><div class="preview__list-wrapper"><div class="preview preview--linear"><div class="preview__wrap"><div class="media-image media-image--1-1 preview__image"><img src="https://sl.sbs.com.au/public/image/file/9953df67-4970-45e3-94df-25cc885f7dab/crop/1x1" alt="Protesters demonstrate at the state Capitol in Harrisburg, Pennsylvania, Monday, April 20, 2020."/></div><div class="preview__content"><h3 class="headline preview__headline"><a href="/news/why-the-far-right-and-militia-groups-are-protesting-coronavirus-lockdowns-in-the-us" title="Why the far-right and militia groups are protesting coronavirus lockdowns in the US">Why the far-right and militia groups are protesting coronavirus lockdowns in the US</a></h3></div></div></div></div></div></p><p>Earlier this week, experts told SBS News <a href="https://www.sbs.com.au/news/why-australian-white-supremacists-and-neo-nazis-are-delighted-with-the-coronavirus-outbreak" target="_blank">some white supremacists and neo-Nazi groups in Australia have welcomed the coronavirus</a>, praising the closing of borders and hoping it will bring about a "societal collapse".&nbsp;</p><p>The World Health Organization has repeatedly referred to the coronavirus outbreak an “infodemic”.</p><p>“We’re not just fighting an epidemic, we’re fighting an infodemic,” WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus told a gathering of security experts in February, where he also said misinformation was spreading “faster and more easily than this virus”.</p><p>The WHO has set up a <a href="https://www.who.int/emergencies/diseases/novel-coronavirus-2019/advice-for-public/myth-busters" target="_blank">“myth busters” page on its website</a> to debunk common COVID-19 misconceptions.</p><p><strong>People in Australia must stay at least 1.5 metres away from others and gatherings are limited to two people unless you are with your family or household.</strong></p><p dir="ltr"><strong>If you believe you may have contracted the virus, call your doctor (don’t visit) or contact the national Coronavirus Health Information Hotline on 1800 020 080. If you are struggling to breathe or experiencing a medical emergency, call 000.</strong></p><p dir="ltr"><strong>SBS is committed to informing Australia’s diverse communities about the latest COVID-19 developments. News and information is available in 63 languages at&nbsp;<a href="https://www.sbs.com.au/coronavirus" target="_blank">sbs.com.au/coronavirus</a>.</strong></p></div>
