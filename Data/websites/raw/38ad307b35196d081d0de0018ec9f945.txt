  <h1>The Netherlands will pay reparations to Indonesian victims of colonial atrocities. Could the UK do the same?</h1><div class="byline">
      By <a href="/news/11029686" target="_self" title="">Max Walden</a></div>
  



  
  
    
      <p class="published">
        Posted 
    <span class="timestamp">
      April 26, 2020 08:06:56
    </span>
      </p>

  <p>Andi Monji was 10 years old when he was forced to watch his father executed in front of him.</p><p>The 83-year-old Indonesian man, who had travelled to The Hague to testify in front of a Dutch court, was last month awarded 10,000 euros ($17,000) as compensation for the murder.</p><p>"His father Mr Monjong was one of more than 200 men to be summarily executed during the purge of the village of Suppa on January 28, 1947," Mr Monji's lawyer Liesbeth Zegveld told the ABC.</p><p>While it has resisted paying reparations, the Dutch Government has formally apologised for the brutal violence it inflicted across the Indonesian archipelago during the 1940s.</p><p>Many atrocities occurred as the Netherlands resisted the creation of an Indonesian nation after founding father Sukarno proclaimed independence on August 17, 1945.</p><p>So what are the international implications of recognising colonial-era massacres, and could similar cases be lodged on behalf of Australian Aboriginal victims of colonial violence?</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg full">
<div class="inner">
  <a href="https://iview.abc.net.au/collection/walking-together" target="_self" title=""><img alt="" class="float--none" xml:lang="none" src="/cm/lb/11984474/data/walking-together-banner-data.jpg"/> </a> </div>
</div><p> </p><h2>Murder in the village of Suppa</h2><p>Dutch attempts to reconquer Indonesia in the 1940s were euphemistically referred to as "police actions" against "terrorists" and nationalist "extremists".</p><p>According to historian Chris Lorenz, "the Dutch government at first tried to represent its last colonial war as a continuation of the Second World War, that is, as a continuing struggle of Dutch democracy against 'fascist' Japan."</p><p>But in reality, the fading Dutch empire was waging counter-guerrilla warfare in a desperate attempt to regain its resource-rich South-East Asian colony.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-26/dutch-soldiers-during-police-action-in-indonesia-in-1947--2/12136260">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/10568074-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Four men in military uniforms poke their heads out of a tank with a portrait of Indonesian leader Soekrano stuck to the front" title="Dutch soldiers during &#039;police action&#039; in Indonesia in 1947" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2020-04-26/dutch-soldiers-during-police-action-in-indonesia-in-1947--2/12136260" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Dutch soldiers in Malang, Indonesia in 1947 as part of "police action". <span class="source">(Dutch National Archives )</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>In Celebes, now the Indonesian province of South Sulawesi, Dutch troops used what would come to be known as the brutal "Westerling method".</p><p>This entailed storming villages and separating men from the women and children. Men suspected of harbouring anti-Dutch attitudes were summarily executed.</p><p>A Dutch inquiry in the 1950s found that more than 3,000 men had been murdered over a three-month period. Indonesian estimates have the figure as much higher.</p><div class="inline-content photo right">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-26/andi-monji-1/12185246">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/12185234-1x1-340x340.jpg" alt="Elderly Indonesian man Andi Monji poses for a photo." title="Andi Monji" width="340" height="340"/>
</a><a href="/news/2020-04-26/andi-monji-1/12185246" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Andi Monji is among Indonesian victims compensated for killings, torture and rapes committed by Dutch forces. <span class="source">(Supplied: Yayasan KUKB)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p><a href="/news/2018-12-09/yaseman-war-crime-case-dutch-army-indonesia-1940s/10551594" target="_self" title="">Mr Monji's case is not the first time Ms Zegveld has achieved justice for victims in Indonesia.</a></p><p>"We have managed to obtain reparations in the form of moral damages for an Indonesian woman who was gang-raped by the Dutch army during the purge of her village in 1949, as well as for an Indonesian man who was tortured whilst in Dutch captivity in 1947," she told the ABC.</p><p>"The colonial times of a country like the Netherlands have, in the past, usually been presented as a source of national pride," Ms Zegveld said.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"I think it is important that the State of the Netherlands takes a long, hard look at its colonial past."</p></blockquote><h2>A history of violence in Indonesia</h2><p>Dutch rule of Indonesia was bloody.</p><p>The 1740 Batavia massacre, referred to in Dutch as Chinezenmoord (murder of the Chinese), saw Dutch soldiers massacre up to an estimated 10,000 ethnic Chinese in what is now Jakarta.</p><div class="inline-content photo right">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-26/map-of-batavia-1/12114968">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/12114954-3x2-340x227.jpg" alt="An historical Dutch map of Batavia, the previous name for today&#039;s Indonesian capital Jakarta." title="Map of Batavia" width="340" height="227"/>
</a><a href="/news/2020-04-26/map-of-batavia-1/12114968" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       An historical Dutch map of Batavia, the previous name for today's Indonesian capital Jakarta. <span class="source">(Supplied: Dutch National Archive)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>In December 1947, Dutch troops killed an estimated 431 people in the village of Rawagede for not revealing the whereabouts of a nationalist leader they were hunting.</p><p>2011 was the first time the Dutch state acknowledged colonial-era atrocities by apologising for the Rawagede massacre.</p><p>"In this context and on behalf of the Dutch government, I apologise for the tragedy that took place in Rawagede on December 9, 1947," said then-ambassador to Indonesia, Tjeerd de Zwaan.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-26/village-massacred-in-aceh-1/12131934">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/12131918-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Villagers massacred by the Dutch lie dead." title="Village massacred in Aceh" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2020-04-26/village-massacred-in-aceh-1/12131934" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       Villagers in Aceh, Indonesia lie massacred during a Dutch expedition in 1904. <span class="source">(Supplied: Tropenmuseum)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>Then in 2013, Mr de Zwaan apologised for all "excesses committed by Dutch forces" between 1945 and 1949 — particularly in Celebes and Rawagede.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"Sometimes it is very important to be able to look back in order to be able to look one another straight in the eyes and be able to move forward together."</p></blockquote><p>In March this year, King Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands expressed regret and apologised "for the excessive violence on the part of the Dutch in those years", referring to the 1940s.</p><p>"I do so with full awareness that the pain and sorrow of the affected families will be felt for generations," the King said during a joint press conference with Indonesian President Joko Widodo.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-26/king-willem-alexander-and-jokowi-1/12114932">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/12114916-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="The Netherland&#039;s King Willem-Alexander and Indonesia&#039;s President Joko Widodo talk to the media." title="King Willem-Alexander and Jokowi" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2020-04-26/king-willem-alexander-and-jokowi-1/12114932" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       King Willem-Alexander apologised for "excessive violence" by Dutch forces during a meeting with Indonesia's President Joko Widodo in March. <span class="source">(Reuters: Sigid Kurniawan via Antara Foto)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><p>"Note that the Dutch king did not apologise for colonialism or even violence, just 'excessive' violence," said Dirk Moses, a professor of genocide history at the University of Sydney.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"So it seems he's not denying the right of the Dutch to reconquer their colonies, just the way they went about it.</p></blockquote><p>"Like the British, the Dutch regarded their rule as enlightened and progressive compared, say, to the Spanish."</p><h2>Right to reparations 'enshrined in international law'</h2><p>Survey data published by pollster YouGov last month found that Dutch and British respondents were the most likely to see former empire more as a source of pride than shame.</p><p>Some 33 per cent of Britons said countries colonised were better off because of colonisation, as opposed to just 17 per cent who said they were worse off.</p><p>But at least <a href="/news/2019-11-18/dozens-of-massacre-sites-added-to-map-of-aboriginal-killings/11707916" target="_self" title="">8,400 mainly Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people were killed by colonists</a> between 1788 and 1930, according to research led by the University of Newcastle.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">

      </a></div><p> </p><p>Asked whether Aboriginal Australians could be entitled to reparation payments from Britain, Ms Zegveld said "absolutely".</p><p>"There are victims alive today who suffered as a result of that violence," she said.</p><p>June Oscar, Australia's Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Social Justice Commissioner, agreed. </p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">

</div><p> </p><p>"The right of Indigenous peoples to remedy and reparation for what they have lost and suffered as a result of colonisation is enshrined in international law," Ms Oscar told the ABC.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"Measures must include public truth-telling, access to justice for victims, and processes for restitution and compensation."</p></blockquote><p>A declaration adopted by the UN General Assembly in 2005 declared the "right to benefit from remedies and reparation" for victims of gross human rights violations.</p><p>This echoed the Rome Statute, which established the International Criminal Court in 1998, declaring principles "relating to reparations to, or in respect of, victims, including restitution, compensation and rehabilitation."</p><h2>Aboriginal children remain 10 times more likely to be taken</h2><p>A spokesperson for the British Foreign and Commonwealth Office told the ABC that "the UK is fully committed to promoting and protecting human rights for all individuals without discrimination on any grounds."</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"There is no disputing that some communities — including indigenous peoples — suffered during colonial times," the spokesperson said.</p></blockquote><p>"We are doing our utmost to address modern day discrimination and intolerance, and ensure that current and future generations do not forget what happened."</p><p>Among the initiatives aimed at engaging Aboriginal Australians is the provision of three postgraduate scholarships to Indigenous candidates per year to study at Cambridge or Oxford Universities, they said.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
