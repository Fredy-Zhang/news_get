    <div class="article-body"><div class="st__content-block st__content-block--list">
    <ul>
        <li>Not enough is known about COVID-19 to predict its future course.</li>
        <li>There is a strong risk that a second spike in infections could overwhelm healthcare systems once lockdowns are lifted.</li>
        <li>Some form of distancing restrictions could be required for more than a year, say experts.</li>
    </ul>
</div>
<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>People across the globe have been forced to adapt quickly to the social distancing brought about by the coronavirus pandemic. But while the world might be eager for a relaxation of the rules, the restrictions might be around for some time to come.</p>
</div>
  <div class="st__content-block st__content-block--searchable-list st__content-block--searchable-list-default list-group" data-gtm-subcontext="body__list">

  </div>
<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>In fact, according to a group of Harvard disease experts, some form of intermittent social distancing may need to be in place until 2022. </p>
</div>
<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>That’s because once the initial wave of COVID-19 infections has passed, further outbreaks could occur. If lockdown restrictions are lifted at the same time, instead of in coordinated phases, a surge in new cases could overwhelm healthcare systems. </p>
</div>
<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p><b>Here to stay</b></p>
</div>
<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>Too little is known about the disease or the future course it will take, prompting <a href="https://science.sciencemag.org/content/early/2020/04/14/science.abb5793" target="_blank" rel="noopener">researchers from the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health</a> to run computer simulations modelling different ways the pandemic could play out. <br><br>With social distancing rules in place and strictly adhered to, along with intensive testing and contact tracing of new infections, the simulations show it is possible to contain the spread of a virus. However, with <a href="https://coronavirus.jhu.edu/map.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">more than 2 million confirmed cases</a> impacting countries around the world, the researchers believe this is an unlikely outcome. <br><br>More likely, they say, is that the virus is here to stay, recurring intermittently as the seasons come and go, much like influenza. Until a vaccine is available, this could mean some form of social distancing will need to exist for many months, if not years.</p>
</div>

<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>At the height of the epidemic in China, strictly enforced lockdown orders were imposed to slow the spread of the disease. <br><br>India has enforced the world’s biggest lockdown, with more than 1.3 billion people confined to their homes. Nationwide restrictions are in place in countries from Europe to Latin America.</p>
</div>
<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>Restrictions range from mandatory quarantine orders to stay-home recommendations, and include prohibiting non-essential work or business, along with bans on public gatherings and events.</p>

        <h4 class="snippet-content__title">What is the World Economic Forum doing about the coronavirus outbreak?</h4>
      </div>
      <div class="snippet-content__body">
        <div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>A new strain of Coronavirus, COVID 19, is spreading around the world, causing deaths and major disruption to the global economy.</p>
</div>
<div class="st__content-block st__content-block--text">
    <p>Responding to this crisis requires global cooperation among governments, international organizations and the <a href="https://www.weforum.org/agenda/2020/03/how-are-companies-responding-to-the-coronavirus-crisis-d15bed6137">business community</a>, which is at the centre of the World Economic Forum’s mission as the International Organization for Public-Private Cooperation. </p>
