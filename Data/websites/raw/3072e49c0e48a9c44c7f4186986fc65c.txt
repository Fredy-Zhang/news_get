<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P>(ATTN: UPDATES throughout with more remarks, details)<br/>By Lee Chi-dong</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> SEOUL, April 27 (Yonhap) -- President Moon Jae-in said Monday his government will move ahead with "realistic and practical" cooperation with North Korea despite international constraints, holding out expectations for a fresh breakthrough in the coronavirus pandemic.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> He was apparently referring to U.N.-led sanctions on Pyongyang as he spoke on the occasion of the second anniversary of his historic Panmunjom summit with North Korean leader Kim Jong-un.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> Moon stressed that South Korea won't sit idle until the external conditions improve, with the Pyongyang-Washington denuclearization talks in a drawn-out stalemate.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> "(We) will explore the most realistic and practical ways for South-North cooperation," he said during a weekly meeting with his senior Cheong Wa Dae aides.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> Moon said the COVID-19 crisis could provide the two Koreas with a new opportunity for bilateral partnerships.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> "For now, it's the most urgent and desperate task," he said. "South and North constitute a single life community, which would serve as the foundation for moving toward a peace community."<br/></P></div></div>

<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> Beginning with anti-coronavirus efforts, the two sides would be able to work together to cope with infectious diseases in animals, disasters in border areas and climate change, Moon added.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> He also said South Korea will do what it can in connection with the project to relink inter-Korean railways.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> Earlier in the day, Seoul's unification ministry in charge of Pyongyang affairs, held its own symbolic ceremony to reaffirm a commitment to the project to modernize and reconnect a pair of railroads across the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ).</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> Moon recalled the moment of briefly stepping over the Military Demarcation Line into the North together with Kim two years earlier. Their first-ever summit took place at the DMZ truce village of Panmunjom, leading to a joint statement on a concerted campaign to bring lasting peace on the peninsula.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> "The Panmunjom Declaration open the door of irreversible peace," but the past two years have shown that peace won't be achieved overnight, Moon said.</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> Little progress so far in implementing the summit accord is not due to the lack of the two Koreas' commitment to actions but because of a failure to overcome the existing international restrictions, he said </P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> "We can't just wait for the conditions to improve," Moon stressed. "We must continue to do even small things that we can do amid realistic constraints."</P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> As soon as the coronavirus is brought under control, Seoul plans to push for the reunification of families living on the other side of the DMZ, and the two sides should continue the joint war remains excavation project, he said. </P></div></div>
<div class="comp-box text-group"><div class="inner"><P> Moon made no comments on media-driven speculation that the North Korean leader, who has gone out of public view for more than two weeks, seems to be critically ill.</P></div></div>
