<div class="comp-rich-text article-text clearfix" id="comp-rich-text11" itemprop=text >
<p>Cattle producers in Central Australia say the fly population there has exploded in the past few months, making working outside almost unbearable.</p><div class="comp-embedded-float-right">
<div class="view-wysiwyg" >
<h2 class="title collectTitle">
Key Points flies in the outback
</h2> <div class="comp-rich-text clearfix" id="comp-rich-text12" >
<h2>Key points:</h2><ul><li>Flies are so bad in Central Australia some cattle producers have bought fly nets for the first time </li><li>One theory is the decline in dung beetles because of the drought has allowed flies to breed</li><li>Some pastoralists are hopeful the fly population explosion is a sign of a good season to come</li></ul> </div>
</div></div><p>Pastoralists are begrudgingly starting to buy fly nets for the first time, a bush fashion faux pas normally associated with overseas tourists visiting the outback.</p><p>At Aileron Station, about 135 kilometres north of Alice Springs, manager Sarah Cook said the sheer number of flies made it difficult to work outside. </p><p>"The flies have been horrendous and so thick," she said. </p><aside class="comp-rich-text-blockquote comp-embedded-float-full source-pullquote" >
<div class="quote">
<p>&quot;You can't talk, you can't breathe with your mouth open, you have to cover your ears in case they get in your ear hole — it's just horrendous.&quot;</p>
</div>
</aside><p>For the first time in her five years at the station the team at Aileron has started to wear fly veils. </p><p>"Everyone's got fly nets and they're a commodity around here," she said. </p><p>"You don't put your net down and leave it unattended because someone else will come and pick it up.</p><p>"Never, ever in our five years have we used a fly net here … and now I've got three."</p><div class="view-image-embed-full" >

</div><h2>'About ten trillion of the bloody things'</h2><p>Chris Nott from Alcoota Station, about 110 kilometres north-east of Alice Springs, has been working in the district since 1983, and said the flies were the worst he had ever seen. </p><p>"Up until last year I reckon I'd hardly ever seen a fly, but I reckon now Alcoota's got about ten trillion of the bloody things," he said. </p><div class="comp-embedded-float-right">
<div class="view-wysiwyg" >
<h2 class="title collectTitle">
Rural Roundup Newsletter
</h2> <div class="comp-rich-text clearfix" id="comp-rich-text17" >
<h2>Our best stories in your inbox? </h2><p><a class="external" href="https://www.abc.net.au/news/rural/newsletters/#roundup" target="_self" title="">Subscribe to Rural RoundUp:</a> Stories from the farm.</p> </div>
</div></div><p>Mr Nott said he too was wearing a fly net for the first time. </p><p>"When I see anyone in my peer group I certainly whip the fly net off and pretend I can handle it, but when there's no one looking I've got it choked up around my neck so that no flies come in," he said. </p><p>Mr Nott thought the flies might have boomed in numbers because the dung beetle population had declined during the drought, giving flies the perfect conditions to breed in. </p><p>"You can see that there's no faeces being buried by dung beetles," he said. </p><div class="view-image-embed-full" >
<div class="comp-lightbox" id="lightbox-18">
