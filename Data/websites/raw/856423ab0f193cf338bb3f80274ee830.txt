<div class="text-abstract">
  
<p class="text-abstract__content">New government estimates reveal that Australia’s catastrophic summer of bushfires released more carbon dioxide emissions than the entire country produces in a year.</p>
</div>
    </div>
  </div>

                      <span>Sam Langford</span>

<div class="text-body"><p dir="ltr"><span>Australia’s summer of bushfires released more than 830 million tonnes of CO2, according to </span><a href="https://www.industry.gov.au/sites/default/files/2020-04/estimating-greenhouse-gas-emissions-from-bushfires-in-australias-temperate-forests-focus-on-2019-20.pdf"><span>new estimates released by the government</span></a><span>.</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>That’s more CO2 than the entire country typically emits in a year -- and Australia already has the 15th highest domestic emissions in the world, out of 193 countries.&nbsp;</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>In fact, the summer’s bushfires released more CO2 than all but five countries; China, the US, India, Russia and Japan.&nbsp;</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>However, the government reports that the greenhouse gas emissions generated by the bushfires will have a “negligible impact” on Australia’s progress towards its 2020 and 2030 emissions reduction targets, as the country’s forests are expected to re-absorb carbon as they recover over the next decade and beyond.</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>As an example, the report cites evidence that more than 98 per cent of forests destroyed during the 2002-03 bushfire season recovered within 10 years.&nbsp;</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>The government reports that the recovery of Australia’s forests following this bushfire season is expected to be complete, “in the absence of new disturbances” such as future bushfires.&nbsp;&nbsp;</span></p><h3 dir="ltr"><strong>The government report suggests the damage done by the 2019-20 bushfire season was more extensive than previously thought.&nbsp;</strong></h3><p dir="ltr"><span>In February, research published in the </span><a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-020-0716-1"><span>journal Nature Climate Change</span></a><span> estimated that between September 2019 and January 2020, fires destroyed around 5.8 million hectares of broadleaf forest, or </span><a href="https://www.sbs.com.au/news/globally-unprecedented-bushfires-burned-more-than-20-per-cent-of-australia-s-forests"><span>more than a fifth of Australia’s forests</span></a><span>.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span><div class="block__container"><div class="label">Read more:</div><div class="preview__list-wrapper"><div class="preview preview--linear"><div class="preview__wrap"><div class="media-image media-image--1-1 preview__image"><img src="https://sl.sbs.com.au/public/image/file/f5ba72b2-e87b-4626-9123-a4e3287bc72c/crop/1x1" alt="Bushfire damage is seen near Tambo Crossing beside the Great Alpine road in the Victorian high country, Saturday, 25 January, 2020."/></div><div class="preview__content"><h3 class="headline preview__headline"><a href="/news/globally-unprecedented-bushfires-burned-more-than-20-per-cent-of-australia-s-forests" title="'Globally unprecedented' bushfires burned more than 20 per cent of Australia's forests">'Globally unprecedented' bushfires burned more than 20 per cent of Australia's forests</a></h3></div></div></div></div></div></span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>The government’s latest report estimates that the current bushfire season affected around 7.4 million hectares of Australian forests up to 11 February 2020, a much higher figure.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span>The graph below shows the area burned this year compared to all previous years dating back to 1851, none of which come close to this year’s damage.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span>&nbsp;<div class="embed embed--image"><img src="https://sl.sbs.com.au/public/image/file/f028ca53-587e-4a96-92e8-22f5de8d3da2"  alt="Annual area burnt by bushfires in Australian temperate forests."   width="700"  height="392"/><div class="attribution">Satellite data: DISER using data supplied by Landgate</div></div></span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>The figure of 7.4 million hectares only takes into account the forested areas burned. Taking into account all land types, </span><a href="https://www.abc.net.au/news/science/2020-03-05/bushfire-crisis-five-big-numbers/12007716"><span>more than 12.6 million hectares</span></a><span> have been burned this fire season.&nbsp;</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>The impacts of such extensive burns go beyond greenhouse gas emissions. Alongside the well-documented economic impact of the fires, estimates by the University of Sydney’s Professor Chris Dickman suggest that the </span><a href="https://www.sydney.edu.au/news-opinion/news/2020/01/08/australian-bushfires-more-than-one-billion-animals-impacted.html"><span>fires killed more than one billion animals nationwide</span></a><span>.</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>Professor Dickman told the </span><span>ABC</span><span> in March that </span><a href="https://www.abc.net.au/news/science/2020-03-05/bushfire-crisis-five-big-numbers/12007716"><span>this estimate was based on a burn area of around 6.25 million hectares</span></a><span>, and is clearly an underestimate now that more extensive burn figures have been released.&nbsp;</span></p><p dir="ltr"><span>“A billion sounds a big number, but it’s a very conservative one,” Professor Dickman said, adding that the figure does not include bats, frogs or fish.&nbsp;</span></p><p><span><span><span>Studies suggest that the summer’s catastrophic bushfires were made at least 30 per cent more likely as </span><a href="https://www.sbs.com.au/news/study-shows-climate-change-fingerprint-in-australian-bushfires"><span>a result of climate change</span></a><span>, and climate scientists have warned that we will face extreme conditions like these more frequently if global temperatures continue to rise. </span></span></span></p></div>
