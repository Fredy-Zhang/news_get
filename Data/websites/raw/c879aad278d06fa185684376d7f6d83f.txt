  <h1>Army veteran calls for access to legal cannabis to help treat post-traumatic stress disorder</h1><div class="byline">
      By <a href="/news/richard-willingham/8654628" target="_self" title="">Richard Willingham</a> and <a href="/news/ben-knight/166868" target="_self" title="">Ben Knight</a></div>

    </span>

  <p>When Max Hill looks you in the eye, he still has the piercing gaze of the drug cop he used to be.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">
<div class="inner">
  <h2>Key points:</h2><ul><li>Veteran David Hill has PTSD and was being treated with benzodiazepines, a minor tranquiliser, at a psychiatric ward</li><li>When he was discharged he deteriorated further as he withdrew from the medication, but medicinal cannabis helped</li><li>When the medicinal product was no longer available, his father Max turned to the black market. The pair now want to help others</li></ul></div>
</div><p> </p><p>Now in retirement, he's still hunting cannabis suppliers. But this time, it's as a customer on behalf of his son, David — an army veteran of two tours of Afghanistan who uses it to treat post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD).</p><p>"I'm a bit slow on the uptake, I suppose," Max said.</p><p>"It took me 22 years in the police force, and 20 years since, to learn that cannabis is a medicine."</p><p>This is no 'Breaking Bad' story. Max is openly and defiantly flouting a law he says has passed its use-by date.</p><p>His turning point came when David — who was suffering terribly with PTSD — left a psychiatric ward after being treated with massive doses of benzodiazepines.</p><p>David said the medication made some symptoms worse.</p><p>"The benzodiazepines were not a positive impact on my life whatsoever," David said.</p><p>"They initially took away the pain. But then it caused addiction, then overt destructive behaviour. It caused me to cut off from other people. It almost exacerbated my symptoms."</p><p>Alone in his apartment, David was deteriorating fast as he tried to come off his medication.</p><p>"He was withdrawing terribly," Max said. </p><p>"Totally dishevelled, crying uncontrollably… incapable of managing anything other than lying in bed and looking at his phone."</p><p>David himself doesn't have much memory of that time, describing himself as a "medicated zombie".</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-25/david-hill-in-afghanistan-1/12183218">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/12183178-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Soldier David Hill sits against a red brick wall and pats a white dog." title="David Hill in Afghanistan" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2020-04-25/david-hill-in-afghanistan-1/12183218" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       David did two tours of Afghanistan during his time serving with the Australian Army. <span class="source">(Supplied: David Hill)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><h2>Cannabis helped ease brutal withdrawal</h2><p>Benzodiazepines, also known as minor tranquillisers, are most commonly prescribed by doctors to relieve stress and anxiety and to help people sleep. </p><p>They can also be used to treat alcohol withdrawal and epilepsy.</p><p>The Australian Alcohol and Drug Foundation says there is increasing concern among medical professionals about the risks of benzodiazepines, particularly when used for a long time. Withdrawal can be brutal.</p><p>And so it was for David Hill. </p><p>A month after discharge from hospital, Max brought his son back to the family home to care for him.</p><p>David had also been prescribed medicinal cannabis to ease his withdrawal.</p><p>At first, it seemed to have little effect.</p><p>"I think we came very, very close to losing him," Max said. </p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"For six long weeks I gave him every meal, every drink, every medication."</p></blockquote><p>Then — quite suddenly — David's condition rapidly improved.</p><p>"He's woken up one day, and we're talking like a light switch. He was back to, as I put it, 93 per cent human again. At his depths, he was about 10 per cent human — if that," Max said.</p><p>What happened next convinced Max it was cannabis making the difference.</p><p>The product they'd been prescribed was no longer available, and the substitute — a much weaker version — wasn't having anything like the same effect.</p><p>He'd also seen research from the US and Israel about how cannabis had been used to treat PTSD there.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-25/david-hill-on-deployment-in-afghanistan-1/12183274">











  
  
  
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
    
  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/12181446-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Soldier David Hill stands on gravel holding a gun, wearing army gear and a helmet." title="David Hill on deployment in Afghanistan" width="700" height="467"/>
</a><a href="/news/2020-04-25/david-hill-on-deployment-in-afghanistan-1/12183274" class="inline-caption"><strong>
        Photo:</strong>
       David believes other veterans with PTSD could benefit from access to subsidised medicinal cannabis. <span class="source">(Supplied: David Hill)</span>
      </a></div><p> </p><h2>Turning to the black market</h2><p>But then, there was the cost — nearly $800 a week. </p><p>David pleaded with the Department of Veterans Affairs to help pay for it, but got nowhere.</p><p>That's when Max — the former drug cop who used to bust bikies selling dope — decided it was time to go to the black market himself.</p><p>"I was beyond caring, frankly," he said.</p><p>"What do you do? DVA wouldn't pay for it, despite repeated requests. They wouldn't even give reasons in writing as to why they wouldn't pay for it."</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">
<div class="inner">
  <h2>If you or anyone you know needs help:</h2><ul><li><a href="http://www.lifeline.org.au/">Lifeline </a>on 13 11 14</li><li><a href="http://www.kidshelp.com.au/">Kids Helpline </a>on 1800 551 800</li><li><a href="http://www.mensline.org.au/Home.html">MensLine Australia </a>on 1300 789 978</li><li><a href="http://www.suicidecallbackservice.org.au/">Suicide Call Back Service </a>on 1300 659 467</li><li><a href="https://www.beyondblue.org.au/" target="_self" title="">Beyond Blue</a> on 1300 224 636</li><li><a href="https://headspace.org.au/" target="_self" title="">Headspace</a> on 1800 650 890</li><li><a href="https://au.reachout.com/" target="_self" title="">ReachOut</a> at <a href="https://au.reachout.com/" target="_self" title="">au.reachout.com</a></li><li><a href="https://clan.org.au/about/" target="_self" title="">Care Leavers Australasia Network (CLAN)</a> on 1800 008 774</li></ul></div>
</div><p> </p><p>The Department of Veterans Affairs spends $230 million a year on mental health support for veterans. </p><p>It has not closed the door on medicinal cannabis, but says right now there is only "limited evidence" supporting the use of the drug for mental health conditions including PTSD.</p><p>"DVA funds treatment where there is a clear evidence base," a spokeswoman said.</p><p>"However, the research into medicinal cannabis as a treatment is rapidly evolving and DVA will continue to monitor the developing research and published evidence."</p><p>The department does have a framework for funding medicinal cannabis for some veterans with chronic pain, chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting, and multiple sclerosis.</p><p>In the meantime, David continues to use black-market cannabis to manage his PTSD symptoms.</p><p>"My anxiety and depression has dwindled down to nearly nothing," he said.</p><p>"I'm sleeping a full night's sleep, waking up fully refreshed with motivation, with a clean mind. It's really nice."</p><div class="inline-content photo full">
    <a href="/news/2020-04-25/young-veteran-david-hill-1/12183304">

</a><a href="/news/2020-04-25/young-veteran-david-hill-1/12183304" class="inline-caption"><strong>

      </a></div><p> </p><h2>Push to legalise, and subsidise, cannabis</h2><p>David Hill's experience is no scientific study.</p><p>But it tallies with results from an open trial of CBD — the therapeutic element in cannabis — on severe anxiety in 30 young people conducted in Melbourne's west.</p><p>Professor Pat McGorry says the trial at Orygen Youth Health found "encouraging results along the lines that David experienced".</p><p>"We now have major funding to test it as a preventive treatment in young people who are in the early stages of psychosis," Professor McGorry said.</p><p>David says he's telling his story on Anzac Day because he's lost too many mates to suicide.</p><p>He fears for those going through the same treatment he did.</p><p>"Talking to them, you notice they're sluggish, unmotivated… it's a walking, unconscious being that you're talking to," David said.</p><p>"That's where I was before this treatment my father found for me. And I just want them to be able to reap the benefits that I have, and see them put their lives back together.</p><blockquote class="quote--pullquote"><p>"There are laws being broken, but they're outdated laws. </p></blockquote><p>"I'd like to see it available, I'd like to see it legalised, I'd like to see it subsidised by the department, and I'd like to see it pushed — because I am a case study. It has worked 100 per cent."</p><p><em>If you or someone you know needs help you can contact Open Arms, a free and confidential counselling service for Australian Defence Force members, veterans and their families, on 1800 011 046.</em></p>
