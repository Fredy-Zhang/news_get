  <h1 class="h1 headline article__headline">Study finds 160 ways to protect the Great Barrier Reef</h1>

<div class="text-abstract">
  
<p class="text-abstract__content">From cloud brightening to heat-tolerant corals, a study has identified 160 possible interventions that could help protect the Great Barrier Reef.</p>
</div>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="row">
    


<div class="meta article__meta">
  <div class="article__meta__byline">
        <div class="meta__item article__meta-date">
      <span class="sr-only">Updated</span> <time itemprop="datePublished" dateTime="2020-04-26T14:03:32+10:00">Updated 1 day ago</time>
    </div>

        <div class="meta__item article__meta-author article__meta-author--block">
        By
                      <span>Peter J Mumby</span>
                        </div>

          </div>

      
  <div class="sharing sharing--outline sharing--context-social article__meta__context-social" data-module="sharing_module">
          

  

  <div class="label sharing__label article__meta__context-social-label">Share</div>

  <div class="row">
    <div class="article__body column">
      

<div class="dfp is-dormant" data-slot-id="slot2" data-module="dfp_module">
  <div class="dfp-slot slot2" id="dfp-slot2" data-lazyload-ads></div>
</div>
      
<div class="text-body"><p>Scientists <a style="font-size: 13.008px;" href="https://theconversation.com/we-just-spent-two-weeks-surveying-the-great-barrier-reef-what-we-saw-was-an-utter-tragedy-135197">recently confirmed</a> the Great Barrier Reef suffered another serious bleaching event last summer - the third in five years. Dramatic intervention to save the natural wonder is clearly needed.</p><p>First and foremost, this requires global greenhouse gas emissions to be slashed. But the right combination of technological and biological interventions, deployed with care at the right time and scale, are also critical to securing the reef’s future.</p><hr /><p><em> <strong> Read more: <a href="https://theconversation.com/we-just-spent-two-weeks-surveying-the-great-barrier-reef-what-we-saw-was-an-utter-tragedy-135197">We just spent two weeks surveying the Great Barrier Reef. What we saw was an utter tragedy</a> </strong> </em></p><hr /><p>This could include methods designed to shade and cool the reef, techniques to help corals adapt to warmer temperatures, ways to help damaged reefs recover, and smart systems that target interventions to the most strategically <a href="https://theconversation.com/how-we-found-112-recovery-reefs-dotted-through-the-great-barrier-reef-88316">beneficial locations</a>.</p><p><a href="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329922/original/file-20200423-47804-61jcir.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=1000&amp;fit=clip"><img src="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329922/original/file-20200423-47804-61jcir.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=754&amp;fit=clip" alt="" /></a> Research into breeding coral hybrids for heat-stress resistance could help restore parts of the reef. Marie Roman/AIMS, Author provided</p><p>Implementing such measures across the breadth of the reef – the world’s biggest reef ecosystem – will not be easy, or cheap. In fact, we believe the scale of the task is greater than the Apollo 11 Moon landing mission in 1969 – but not impossible.</p><p>That mission was a success, not because a few elements worked to plan, but because of the integration, coordination and alignment of every element of the mission’s goal: be the first to land and walk on the Moon, and then fly home safely.</p><p>Half a century later, facing the ongoing decline of the Great Barrier Reef, we can draw important lessons from that historic human achievement.</p><h2>Intervening to save the reef</h2><p>The recently released <a href="https://www.gbrrestoration.org/investment-case-reports">Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program concept feasibility study</a> shows Australia could feasibly, and with reasonable probability of success, intervene to help the reef adapt to and recover from the effects of climate change.</p><p>The study, of which we were a part, involved more than 100 leading coral reef scientists, modellers, economists, engineers, business strategists, social scientists, decision scientists and reef managers.</p><p><a href="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329923/original/file-20200423-47820-jfi9bw.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=1000&amp;fit=clip"><img src="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329923/original/file-20200423-47820-jfi9bw.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=754&amp;fit=clip" alt="" /></a> More than 100 coral reef scientists took part in the feasibility study. Nick Thake/AIMS, Author provided</p><p>It shows how new and existing interventions, supported by the best available research and development, could help secure a future for the reef.</p><p>We must emphasise that interventions to help the reef adapt to and recover from climate change will not, alone, save it. Success also depends on reducing global greenhouse emissions as quickly as possible. But the hands-on measures we’re proposing could help buy time for the reef.</p><h2>Cloud brightening to heat-tolerant corals</h2><p>Our study identified 160 possible interventions that could help revive the reef, and build on its natural resilience. We’ve whittled it down to the 43 most effective and realistic.</p><p>Possible interventions for further research and development include <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2020/apr/16/brightening-clouds-and-coral-larvae-study-picks-best-great-barrier-reef-rescue-ideas">brightening clouds</a> with salt crystals to shade and cool corals; ways to <a href="https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/ece3.5616">increase the abundance</a> of naturally heat-tolerant corals in local populations, such as through aquarium-based selective breeding and release; and methods to promote faster recovery on damaged reefs, such as deploying structures designed to stabilise reef rubble.</p><p>But there will be no single silver bullet solution. The feasibility study showed that methods working in combination, along with water quality improvement and crown-of-thorns starfish control, will provide the best results.</p><p><a href="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329924/original/file-20200423-47832-mvqqwg.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=1000&amp;fit=clip"><img src="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329924/original/file-20200423-47832-mvqqwg.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=754&amp;fit=clip" alt="" /></a> Field testing the heat resistant coral hybrids in the Great Barrier Reef. Kate Green/AIMS, Author provided</p><h2>Harder than landing on the Moon</h2><p>There are four reasons why saving the Great Barrier Reef in coming decades could be more challenging than the 1969 Moon mission.</p><p>First, warming events have already driven the reef into decline with <a href="https://theconversation.com/back-to-back-bleaching-has-now-hit-two-thirds-of-the-great-barrier-reef-76092">back-to-back bleaching</a> events in 2016 and 2017, and now again in 2020. The next major event is now only just around the corner.</p><hr /><p><em> <strong> Read more: <a href="https://theconversation.com/this-situation-brings-me-to-despair-two-reef-scientists-share-their-climate-grief-123520">'This situation brings me to despair': two reef scientists share their climate grief</a> </strong> </em></p><hr /><p>Second, current <a href="https://theconversation.com/paris-climate-targets-arent-enough-but-we-can-close-the-gap-61798">emission reduction pledges</a> would see the world warm by <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/nature18307">2.3-3.5℃ relative to pre-industrial levels</a>. This climate scenario, which is <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-00177-3">not the worst case</a>, would be beyond the range that allows today’s coral reef ecosystems to function.</p><p>Without swift action, the prospect for the world’s coral reefs is bleak, with most expected to <a href="https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fmars.2017.00158/full">become seriously degraded</a> before mid-century.</p><p><a href="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329925/original/file-20200423-47788-cro8pt.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=1000&amp;fit=clip"><img src="https://images.theconversation.com/files/329925/original/file-20200423-47788-cro8pt.jpg?ixlib=rb-1.1.0&amp;q=45&amp;auto=format&amp;w=754&amp;fit=clip" alt="" /></a> The Great Barrier Reef has been hit by consecutive bleaching events – restoring it may be harder than landing on the moon. Shutterstock</p><p>Third, we still have work to do to control local pressures, including <a href="https://theconversation.com/great-barrier-reef-report-to-un-shows-the-poor-progress-on-water-quality-69779">water quality</a> and marine pests <a href="https://theconversation.com/how-scaring-starfish-could-help-to-save-the-great-barrier-reef-36759">crown-of-thorns starfish</a>.</p><p>And fourth, the inherent <a href="https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/conl.12050">complexity of natural systems</a>, particularly ones as diverse as coral reefs, provides an additional challenge not faced by NASA engineers 50 years ago.</p><p>So keeping the Great Barrier Reef, let alone the rest of the world’s reefs, safe from climate change will dwarf the challenge of any space mission. But there is hope.</p><h2>We must start now</h2><p>The federal government <a href="https://minister.awe.gov.au/ley/media-releases/150-million-drive-innovations-boost-reef-resilience">recently re-announced</a> A$100 million from the Reef Trust Partnership towards a major research and development effort for this program. This will be augmented by contributions of A$50m from research institutions, and additional funding from international philanthropists.</p><p>Our study shows that under a wide range of future emission scenarios, the program is very likely to be worth the effort, more so if the world meets the Paris target and rapidly cuts greenhouse gas emissions.</p><hr /><p><em> <strong> Read more: <a href="https://theconversation.com/i-studied-what-happens-to-reef-fish-after-coral-bleaching-what-i-saw-still-makes-me-nauseous-134247">I studied what happens to reef fish after coral bleaching. What I saw still makes me nauseous</a> </strong> </em></p><hr /><p>What’s more, economic analyses included in the feasibility study show successful Great Barrier Reef intervention at scale could create benefits to Australia of between A$11 billion and A$773 billion over a 60-year period, with much of it flowing to regional economies and Traditional Owner communities.</p><p>And perhaps more importantly, if Australia is successful in this effort, we can lead the world in a global effort to save these natural wonders bequeathed to us across the ages. We must start the journey now. If we wait, it may be too late.</p><hr /><p><em>The authors gratefully acknowledge the contribution of David Wachenfeld, Chief Scientist of the Great Barrier Reef Marine Park Authority and member of the the steering committee for the development of this program.</em></p><p><img src="https://counter.theconversation.com/content/121052/count.gif?distributor=feed-factiva" alt="The Conversation" width="1" height="1" /></p><p class="fine-print"><em>Paul Hardisty is CEO of AIMS. AIMS has received funding funding from the Commonwealth Government for the Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program feasibility study.</em></p><p class="fine-print"><em>Christian Roth has received funding from CSIRO and the Commonwealth Government for the Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program feasibility study, as well as being part-time seconded to the Great Barrier Reef Foundation.</em></p><p class="fine-print"><em>Damien Burrows works for James Cook University, has received funding from the Commonwealth government for reef research and manages staff who have received funding from the Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program. </em></p><p class="fine-print"><em>David Mead works for AIMS. AIMS has received funding funding from the Commonwealth Government for the Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program feasibility study.</em></p><p class="fine-print"><em>Ken Anthony receives funding from the Australian Government for research conducted at the Australian Institute of Marine Science</em></p><p class="fine-print"><em>Line K Bay receives funding from AIMS, the Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program, the Great Barrier Reef Foundation, the National Environment Science Program and the Agouron Institute.</em></p><p class="fine-print"><em>Mark Gibbs receives funding from The Reef Restoration and Adaptation Program, Great Barrier Reef Foundation, and Australian Institute of Marine Sciences. Mark also sits on the Board of Reef Check Australia.</em></p><p class="fine-print"><em>Peter J Mumby works for the University of Queensland and devotes a proportion of his time to the Great Barrier Reef Foundation as Chief Scientist. He is funded by the Commonwealth and Queensland governments for research into coral reef ecology and management. This includes funding for the Reef Restoration and Adaptation Programme. </em></p></div>
