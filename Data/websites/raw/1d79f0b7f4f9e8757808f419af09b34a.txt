	<h1>Scientists fear the hunt for a coronavirus vaccine will fail and we will all have to live with the 'constant threat' of COVID-19</h1>

<div class="display-media image primary centre clear">
<a href="https://i.insider.com/5e7742402d654f16522f2b83" rel="modal"><amp-img src="https://i.insider.com/5e7742402d654f16522f2b83" alt="" height="512" width="1024" layout="responsive" class="primary"></amp-img></a><span class="image_source">Getty</span>
</div>
<ul>
<li>
    <strong>Some scientists fear that an <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/coronavirus-vaccine-timeline-what-to-watch-leading-research-2020-3">effective coronavirus vaccine</a> may prove impossible to produce.</strong>
  </li>
<li>
    <strong>The UK’s Chief Medical Officer warned on Friday that there is “concerning” evidence suggesting that people can be reinfected with the virus.</strong>
  </li>
<li>
    <strong>He said evidence from other forms of coronavirus also suggests that immunity quickly wanes.</strong>
  </li>
<li>
    <strong>No vaccine has ever been approved for use against previous forms of coronavirus.</strong>
  </li>
<li>
    <strong>David Nabarro, professor of global health at Imperial College, said the world may have to learn to live with the “constant threat” of COVID-19.</strong>
  </li>
<li>
    <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/?hprecirc-bullet">Visit Business Insider’s homepage for more stories</a>.
  </li>
</ul>
<hr>
<p>Scientists fear that it may prove impossible to produce a <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/coronavirus-vaccine-timeline-what-to-watch-leading-research-2020-3"> working coronavirus vaccine</a> and believe the world may have to simply learn to adapt to the <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/coronavirus-vaccine-timeline-challenges-and-progress-2020-4"> permanent threat</a> of COVID-19.</p>
<p>The UK’s Chief Medical Officer, Christopher Whitty, told a Parliamentary committee on Friday that there was “concerning” evidence suggesting that it may not be possible to stimulate immunity to the virus.</p>
<p>“The first question we do not know is ‘do you get natural immunity to this disease if you have had it, for a prolonged period of time?'” Whitty said.</p>
<p>“Now if we don’t then it doesn’t make a vaccine impossible but it makes it much less likely and we simply don’t know yet.</p>
<p>He said there was “a little bit of evidence that some people have been reinfected with this having had a previous infection.”</p>
<p>He added: “That’s a slightly concerning situation.”</p>
<p>  <em><br><strong>Read more: <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/coronavirus-vaccine-timeline-what-to-watch-leading-research-2020-3">There are more than 70 potential coronavirus vaccines in the works. Here are the top efforts to watch, including the 16 vaccines set to be tested in people this year.</a></strong><br></em></p>
<h2>No coronavirus vaccine has ever been produced</h2>
<div class="display-media image primary centre clear">
<a href="https://i.insider.com/5ea0201ce61f34592d164ab4" rel="modal"><amp-img src="https://i.insider.com/5ea0201ce61f34592d164ab4" alt="Coronavius vaccine roche 2021" height="512" width="1024" layout="responsive" class="primary"></amp-img></a><span class="image_source">Getty</span>
</div>
<p><a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/coronavirus-vaccine-timeline-prospects-can-we-make-one-2020-4">Doubts about the possibility of a viable vaccine</a> are based largely on the fact that no vaccine has ever been approved for use in the US or UK against other forms of coronavirus.</p>
<p>Whitty told the committee the evidence from other forms of coronavirus was that “immunity [to the virus] wanes relatively quickly.”</p><amp-ad 
  width=300
  height=250
  type="doubleclick"
  data-slot="/1027487/mobile-businessinsider-amp"
  data-multi-size="320x50"
  data-multi-size-validation="false"
  json='{"targeting":{"pos":["1"], "ctype": ["article"], "MVPropertyID": ["NA-BUSIINSIAMP-11239097"], "pageID": 2092691, "cat": "briefing", "aggregate":["coronavirus", "coronavirus-vacccine", "healthcare", "international", "pandemic", "politics", "quarantine", "science-us", "uk-weekend"]}}'>
</amp-ad>
<p>He said that the world needs “to be careful that we don’t assume that we are going to have a vaccine for this disease as we have had for, let’s say measles, which once you have it you’re protected for life.”</p>
<p>“We cannot guarantee success,” he added.</p>
<p>“Vaccines are looked for, for every infectious disease, they are not found for all of them.”</p>
<p>  <em><br><strong>Read more: <a href="https://www.businessinsider.com/wuhan-coronavirus-treatments-vaccine-jj-regeneron-gilead-2020-2">The US is sprinting to develop a coronavirus vaccine or treatment. Here’s how 19 top drugmakers are racing to tackle the pandemic.</a></strong><br></em></p>
<p>The World Health Organisation on Saturday also threw doubt on the possibility that immunity to the virus could be induced.</p>
<p>In a statement about plans by some governments to <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com.au/uk-plans-coronavirus-immunity-passports-so-brits-can-leave-lockdown-2020-4"> introduce so-called “immunity passports,” for those previously infected with the virus</a> the organisation said in a statement that: “there is currently no evidence that people who have recovered from COVID-19 and have antibodies are protected from a second infection.”</p>
<p>Other scientists have also raised the possibility that a working virus may never emerge to deal with COVID-19.</p>
<p><a href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2020/apr/18/how-did-britain-get-its-response-to-coronavirus-so-wrong">In an interview with The Observer</a> David Nabarro, professor of global health at Imperial College, London said the world had to realise that a vaccine may not be possible.</p>
<p>“You don’t necessarily develop a vaccine that is safe and effective against every virus,” he told the paper.</p>
<p>“Some viruses are very, very difficult when it comes to vaccine development – so for the foreseeable future, we are going to have to find ways to go about our lives with this virus as a constant threat.”</p>
<p>Even if a fully effective vaccine proves impossible, Whitty believes that a partially effective vaccine would still be worth pursuing.</p>
<p>“You can have vaccines that are not capable of providing [high levels of] immunity, but they provide enough protection that people don’t get severe disease.</p>
<p>“So we might get a vaccine that is rather less effective but is sufficiently effective, that if we vaccinated everyone at a high level of dying from this… we might well be able to massively reduce fatalities even if there was still natural infections.”</p>
